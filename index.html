Route overview

21 days, one base, three trips out

The one decision that shapes everything: Caribbean on the first weekend, Pacific on the second. The southern Caribbean coast gets an extension of its October dry window in early November, then turns wet from mid-month. The Pacific side does the opposite — it dries out steadily all November. Flipping the order costs nothing and buys better weather on both coasts. Ticos: if this contradicts what you know from living here, say so — you win that argument.

7:00 – 12:00

Parque Nacional Manuel Antoniocapped entrymust

The smallest national park in the country and the most densely populated by animals: two-toed sloths, capuchins, howlers, agoutis and, with luck, squirrel monkeys, which only live along this coast. Book online in advance — the daily visitor cap is genuinely enforced. The park closes one day a week, so confirm Sunday before booking.

Watch the capuchins: they open backpacks and steal food. Do not feed them and do not leave a bag unattended.

🚗 Local driving specifics

  • Addresses don't exist. Directions run off landmarks: "200 metres north of the church, 50 east." Waze is the local standard and handles this better than Google Maps.
  • Attended fuel stations — you don't pump it yourself. State an amount or say "lleno", and check the meter is zeroed.
  • Tolls on Ruta 27 are cash only in colones. Keep coins in the car.
  • Police checkpoints near national parks are routine, not a sign of trouble. Licence, IDP, passport or a copy.
  • Night driving outside the highways is genuinely worse than the European equivalent: unlit, unshouldered, and shared with pedestrians and dogs. Worth building into the day plan rather than the driving.
  • Ruta 32 is the one road that can be closed outright. Check before committing to it.

🪪 Paperwork

  • Visa: not required for Polish citizens up to 90 days.
  • Onward ticket — immigration may ask for it, so carry a printout.
  • IDP: not legally required under 90 days, but a Polish licence is in neither Spanish nor English, which technically puts it in the "translation recommended" category. Jumbo Car's own terms need checking — local rental companies sometimes require one. From PZM in Poland: 60–100 zł, one day.
  • Insurance: Revolut Metal. Two things to verify — the single-trip limit (21 days) and whether the plan includes car hire excess, which would cover the $1,200 deductible.
  • Vaccinations: none required when arriving from Europe.

💳 Money

  • Currency: colón (CRC). Dollars are accepted almost everywhere, but the shop rate is always worse than the bank rate.
  • Cards work in cities and tourist areas. Cash is needed for sodas, markets, tolls, guides and park donations.
  • Revolut withdrawing from a BAC or BCR ATM works best. Always decline when an ATM offers to convert to złoty.
  • Tipping: a 10% service charge is already on restaurant bills. Anything extra is a gesture, not an obligation.
  • Car deposit: roughly $1,200 held on the card — check the limit and tell the bank about the trip.

🔌 Tech

  • Type A/B sockets, 120 V — Polish plugs don't fit. Bring adapters, ideally two. Most USB chargers handle 100–240 V, but check each device.
  • SIM/eSIM: Kölbi (ICE) has the best coverage outside cities; Claro and Movistar are cheaper. eSIM can be bought before departure.
  • Signal disappears in Monteverde and in patches along the Caribbean. Download offline maps for the whole country.
  • Emergency number: 911, English-capable.

🍽️ What to eat

  • Gallo pinto — rice and beans, the national breakfast, with egg and natilla.
  • Casado — the lunch plate: rice, beans, salad, plátano maduro, a protein.
  • Olla de carne — thick beef and root-vegetable stew, a Sunday classic.
  • Chifrijo — rice, beans, chicharrón and pico de gallo in one bowl, made for beer.
  • Ceviche de corvina — milder than the Peruvian version, heavy on cilantro.
  • Rice and beans and rondón — the Caribbean versions, cooked in coconut milk. Different league.
  • Salsa Lizano — on every table, ends up on everything.
  • To drink: Imperial, agua de sapo, café chorreado, guaro Cacique with lime.
  • A soda is a small family kitchen. Eat there, not at places with English menus and photos of the food.

🌦️ November weather

  • Central Valley (Heredia): 18–26°C, afternoon showers, mornings usually clear.
  • Caribbean: early November extends the October dry window; from mid-month it turns distinctly wet. Hence the trip on the first weekend.
  • Pacific: dries out through November — the second half is noticeably better. Hence Manuel Antonio in week two.
  • Monteverde: rain and cloud all month, 14–18°C. Bring a warm layer.
  • November is the end of the green season and the start of high season — fewer people and lower prices than December.

🛡️ Safety

  • Nothing in the car. Not on the seat, not in the trunk. Break-ins at beach and trailhead parking are the main crime affecting visitors.
  • San José after dark — stick to Escalante and Sabana, avoid the Coca-Cola area and the Zona Roja.
  • Rip currents on Pacific beaches are a real hazard and lifeguards are rare. If caught, swim parallel to shore.
  • Mosquitoes: dengue is present, especially on the coasts. DEET repellent, long sleeves at dusk.
  • Tap water is drinkable in the Central Valley and most of the country.

🎁 What to take home

  • Coffee — Tarrazú, Monteverde, Doka. Buy at the farm or in a supermarket, not at the airport.
  • Salsa Lizano — several bottles, the best dollar-a-piece gift there is.
  • Chocolate from the Caribbean — Caribeans in Puerto Viejo, cacao from Talamanca.
  • Guaro Cacique — the national sugarcane spirit.
  • A miniature carreta from Sarchí — pocket-sized UNESCO heritage.
  • Café Britt — practically in Heredia, convenient for the last minute.
